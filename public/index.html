🔒 What just happened? — How hashing works

When you type or pick your birthday, your browser instantly runs it through a hash function (SHA-256). The result is a fixed-length string of letters and numbers — the hash. This hash is what gets sent to the server. Your actual birthday never leaves your device.

🐄 Think of it like turning a cow into a burger: easy to go from cow → burger, but impossible to reconstruct the cow from the burger. Hashing works the same way — it\'s a one-way mathematical function. No matter how long you stare at the hash, you can\'t reverse-engineer the original password from it.

But wait — birthdays are a weak password!
Because there are only ~36,500 possible birthdays in a 100-year range, an attacker can simply try all of them, hash each one, and compare. That\'s exactly what the instructor\'s dashboard does — in seconds. This is called a brute-force attack, and it shows why predictable passwords (like birthdays) are insecure even when "protected" by hashing.
